§1Executive summary

Real DDR/HBM controllers converge on a small set of documented patterns:

  • Out-of-order scheduling over a reorder/CAM structure. Synopsys uMCTL2 (DDR) exposes up to 64 read + 64 write look-ahead with a read reorder buffer; the Synopsys HBM3 controller uses CAM-based scheduling, depth 32 or 64. The policy is FR-FCFS-like, and page policy is programmable (open vs close-with-timer) — that is the row-buffer-locality vs bank-level-parallelism knob.
  • Per-channel / per-pseudo-channel replication. HBM stacks are a 1024-bit interface split into 16 independent 64-bit channels / 32 32-bit pseudo-channels with independent (asynchronous) clocks, so the controller is instantiated per (pseudo)channel. AMD/Xilinx's HBM2 IP: one 2-channel MC per 2 pseudo-channels (16 MCs for 32 PCs) behind a segmented AXI crossbar.
  • Latency lives in the front-end/crossbar + scheduler + DFI/PHY. AMD publishes 90 vs 108 memory-clocks (open-page vs activate, direct path); a measurement on Alveo U280 shows ~229 ns round-trip read (~182 ns in HBMC+HBM), with the AXI crossbar the main adder.
  • Ramulator 2.0 is the authoritative open reference and is itself validated by replaying its issued command trace into Micron's DDR4 Verilog model — the same method this project uses against Ramulator.

§2Commercial controller IP — documented microarchitecture

2.1 Synopsys DesignWare uMCTL2 (DDR) vendor / 3-0

The Universal DDR Memory Controller documents configurable out-of-order scheduling with up to 64 read and 64 write look-ahead commands, dual address queues per port, QoS, variable-priority commands, and a Read Reorder Buffer (RRB) giving out-of-order execution with in-order responses. It accepts 1–16 host ports over AMBA AXI4/AXI/AHB or a custom single-port ultra-low-latency H-IF. [uMCTL2 page] [read-reorder WP]

Maps to our design: the RRB / look-ahead window is the industrial analog of our FR-FCFS arbiter scanning for the oldest ready command; "in-order responses" is why real controllers carry request IDs (our seq/id).

2.2 Synopsys DesignWare HBM3 controller vendor / 3-0

Documented features: Dynamic Memory Access Scheduling (CAM-based) with CAM size 32 or 64; page policy (Open, or Close-with-timer); instantiated per pseudo-channel (up to 32 PCs, 16–64 banks/PC); a multiport AMBA (4 AXI) interface with managed QoS per PC. It implements a DFI 5.0-compliant interface to the HBM3 PHY at up to 6.4 Gbps in DFI 1:1:2 with a 1.6 GHz controller clock (controller/DFI/PHY/memory all 1.6 GHz, 3.2 GHz strobe; DFI 1:1 CA, 1:2 data). Higher DFI ratios reach 7.2 Gbps on the PHY. [HBM3 controller] [What is HBM3]

Maps to our design: "CAM-based scheduling, page policy = open" is exactly the open-row FR-FCFS our controller implements; "per pseudo-channel instantiation" matches our single-channel × 2-pseudochannel scope; "DFI 5.0 to PHY" is the datapath our pipelined variant models as the registered egress command pipeline.

§3HBM structure & why controllers are replicated per channel vendor / 3-0

HBM3 keeps a 1024-bit data interface, divided into 16 independent 64-bit channels, each with two pseudo-channels (32-bit DDR each) → 32 pseudo-channels; this doubles independent channels from 8 (HBM2) to 16. Crucially, channels operate with independent clocks and need not be synchronous — the architectural reason the controller is replicated per (pseudo)channel. HBM3 also mandates strong, symbol-based on-die ECC (a fixed 304-bit code word: 272 data + 32 check) plus real-time error reporting (on-die ECC was optional in HBM2). [JEDEC JESD238] [Synopsys glossary]

Our 2-pseudochannel, 4-bankgroup, 4-bank (32-bank) HBM3 organization and per-bank timing are the modeled subset of one such channel.

§4AMD/Xilinx HBM2 IP — a fully concrete real architecture vendor / 3-0

The AMD/Xilinx AXI HBM hard IP (Alveo U280/U50, documented in PG276) is the most completely public real HBM controller architecture:

  • Each stack = 8 channels × 2 (64-bit) pseudo-channels = 16 PCs per 1024-bit stack; each PC limited to its own 1/16 of the stack (256 MB).
  • One 2-channel memory controller per 2 PCs → 16 MCs for 32 PCs on a 2-stack (8 GB) board.
  • One AXI port per pseudo-channel (16 ports/stack) through a 16×16 AXI crossbar (32×32 for two stacks), so any master can reach any PC via global addressing.
  • The crossbar is hierarchical — eight 4×4 unit switches, not one monolithic 32×32. Aligning a master to its local PC (M0→S0 for 0–256 MB) avoids lateral inter-switch traversal; crossing all 32 channels was measured at <0.5 GB/s (>20× bandwidth loss).

[AMD PG276] [Vitis "Using HBM"] [arXiv 2010.06075] [Shuhai 2005.04324] [ScalaBFS 2105.11754]

Maps to our design: "one MC per 2 PCs" is precisely the granularity our single-channel controller models; the AXI crossbar is the front-end stage our controller (and Ramulator) abstracts away — and a real source of latency (next).

§6Page policy & address mapping: the locality vs parallelism knob

Both vendor controllers expose page policy as a first-class option (Synopsys HBM3: Open or Close-with-timer). This is the industrial form of the row-buffer-locality vs bank-level-parallelism tradeoff: open page keeps rows open to harvest hits (streaming/locality); closed page precharges eagerly to expose bank parallelism (scattered access). Address mapping is the second knob — Ramulator 2 ships mappers like MOP4CLXOR (permuting bank/channel bits low to spread accesses) vs row-high mappers (our RoBaRaCoCh) that preserve row locality. vendor / 3-0 for the page-policy options; background for the quantitative tradeoff (established by the scheduling literature, §8). [Synopsys HBM3]

§7Ramulator 2.0 — the open reference, and how our controller relates paper / 3-0

Ramulator 2.0 is the authoritative open, modular DRAM simulator. Its controller decomposes into shared interfaces + independent implementations: frontend → address mapper (MOP4CLXOR) → generic DRAM controller → request scheduler (FR-FCFS) → DRAM device model → refresh manager (all-bank), plus optional plugins (e.g. PARA RowHammer mitigation). Per cycle it ticks the refresh manager, queries the scheduler (which consults the DRAM device model to decode the best command), then issues. It covers DDR3/4/5, LPDDR5, HBM2/HBM3, GDDR6 via reusable templated lambdas, and is validated by replaying its issued command trace into Micron's DDR4 Verilog model with identical organization/timings — finding no timing or state-transition violations. [Ramulator 2 paper] [PDF]

Maps directly to this project: our 04_hbm_rtl controller mirrors this exact decomposition (frontend phases, addr_mapper, frfcfs_arbiter, bank_state + timing_engine device model, refresh), and we validate it the same way Ramulator validates against Micron — by diffing the issued command trace — except our golden is Ramulator's own TraceRecorder rather than a Verilog DRAM model. We extended Ramulator's validation methodology one level up: behavioral model → synthesizable RTL.

§10Limitations & open questions

Confidence / scope caveats

  1. The richest microarch detail (look-ahead/CAM depth, page policy, ports, DFI ratios) is from vendor product pages stating configurable "up to" maxima — not measured runtime behavior or internal pipeline-stage breakdowns. True per-stage depth inside the IP is proprietary.
  2. The only concrete latency numbers are AMD/Xilinx's 90/108 mem-clocks (config-dependent) and the Alveo U280 229 ns / 182 ns measurement — both older Xilinx HBM2 hard IP; they do not generalize to Versal HBM, discrete-GPU HBM, or CPU/GPU IMCs.
  3. Not gathered/verified this pass: Cadence/Denali, Rambus, ARM CMN/DMC microarchitecture; refresh specifics (per-bank refresh, postponement, DRFM/RFM granularity); write CRC; CA parity; link-ECC; and the scheduling research literature beyond names. Absence ≠ undocumented.
  4. The 90/108-clock finding passed 2-1 (the dissent was a config-dependence caveat, not a factual refutation) — vendor-published but configuration-specific. All other findings are 3-0 with primary sources.

Open questions for a follow-up pass

  • Public/microbenchmark latency breakdowns of CPU/GPU integrated memory controllers (Intel/AMD IMC, NVIDIA GPU HBM), split into front-end/CDC vs scheduler vs DFI/PHY.
  • Cadence/Denali, Rambus, ARM reorder-depth / scheduling / refresh comparison.
  • Empirical row-buffer-locality vs bank-level-parallelism results from FR-FCFS/PAR-BS/TCM/BLISS/USIMM, and how CAM-based vendor schedulers map onto them.
  • Concrete advanced refresh/RAS implementation (per-bank refresh, postponement, DRFM/RFM, RowHammer-aware, write CRC, CA parity, on-die/link ECC) in shipping controllers.
